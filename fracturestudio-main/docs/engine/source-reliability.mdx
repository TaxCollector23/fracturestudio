title: "Source Reliability"
description: "How to read the source results and what they do and do not mean."
---

The source check is a starting-point finder, not a fact-checker. Understanding what it measures keeps you from over-trusting a match.

## What a match means

A match is based on how closely your claim's distinctive terms appear on the retrieved page. A high match means the page discusses the same subject in similar terms — not that the page proves your specific claim.

## What it does not mean

- It does not confirm the page is accurate or authoritative.
- It does not confirm the page actually supports the exact conclusion you attached to it.
- It does not check the methodology or credibility of a study it finds.

## Reading results well

- Treat **likely supported** as "worth opening," not "verified."
- For any claim that carries weight in your argument, open the source and read the relevant passage yourself.
- Prefer primary sources, official data, and reputable publications over aggregators.
- If a claim is flagged **citation incomplete** or **vague**, name the source precisely in your draft and run the check again.

<Note>
A reputable source can be real and still fail to prove the conclusion attached to it. The engine separates "is this source real" from "does it actually support this claim."
</Note>
